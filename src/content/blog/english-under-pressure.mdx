There's a version of your English you're happy with. You use it in informal meetings, familiar contexts, conversations where you're comfortable. You're fluent, precise, and often compelling.

Then there's your high-stakes English: investor calls, board presentations, difficult negotiations. And if you're honest, it's a different thing entirely. Slower. Vaguer. Less like you.

This is one of the most common experiences for advanced non-native speakers, and the least discussed — perhaps because it's uncomfortable to admit. But it's not a language problem. It's a performance problem. And performance problems have performance solutions.

## What Actually Happens Under Pressure

The cognitive science here is reasonably well understood. When you're under stress, the brain allocates resources toward the perceived threat and away from the higher-order processes that support complex language production.

For a second-language user, this hits harder than for a native speaker, because second-language production requires more cognitive overhead in the first place. Vocabulary retrieval, register monitoring, structural planning — these are slightly more effortful processes than they are in your native language. Under pressure, that extra effort becomes untenable, and the system simplifies.

The result: shorter sentences. More hedging. Simpler vocabulary. Slower retrieval. The precise, complex English you use when comfortable becomes the safe, hedged English you use when anxious.

## The Three Most Common Patterns

**Hedge stacking.** Pressure produces hedging. Individual hedges aren't a problem — they serve legitimate communicative functions. But under stress they stack: "Maybe we could potentially sort of consider looking at..." Each word individually is defensible; together they erode credibility. You sound uncertain about your own business, your own plan, your own position.

**Structural collapse.** Complex, well-structured arguments require planning capacity. Under pressure, that capacity gets squeezed. Sentences start without knowing how they'll end. Points are added after the fact ("Oh, and also...") rather than sequenced intentionally. The result is hard to follow and leaves an impression of disorganisation — even when the underlying thinking is sound.

**Native language intrusion.** Certain structures from your first language become more dominant when cognitive resources are depleted. These aren't always grammatically wrong in English, but they can produce unusual rhythms or turns of logic that don't match the pattern your English-speaking audience expects. This creates vague unease without a clear cause — and the listener can't tell you what's off.

## The Gap Between Preparation and Performance

The usual advice is to practise more and gain confidence. This is partially true and mostly insufficient.

The problem isn't a lack of English practice — most people at this level have already done enormous amounts of that. The problem is a lack of *performance* practice: deliberate exposure to high-pressure English in conditions that approximate the real thing closely enough to build genuine resilience.

Reading English, studying English, even speaking English casually doesn't prepare you for the moment an investor asks a question you weren't expecting, or a board member challenges your numbers in public, or a negotiation reaches an impasse and the room goes quiet and everyone is looking at you.

What prepares you for those moments is practice in those moments — or the closest simulation you can create.

## What Actually Builds Performance Fluency

### Simulate the actual conditions

Not just the topic — the conditions. Real-time, without notes. With someone asking difficult follow-up questions. On video or in person, not in a chat window. With time pressure if time pressure is the real challenge.

The simulation needs to be uncomfortable to be useful. If you're sailing through practice, you're practising the wrong thing. The discomfort you feel in practice is the signal that you're in the range where real improvement happens.

### Prepare anchors, not scripts

Scripts don't survive contact with reality. A carefully rehearsed answer falls apart the moment the question is slightly different from what you expected — which it always is.

What helps is a set of anchors: the three things you must communicate, the opening line of your response to the hardest likely question, the closing move you want to make. Anchors are stable under pressure in a way that detailed scripts are not. They give you something to return to when the thread gets lost.

### Build a recovery repertoire

The moment of breakdown — the pause that goes too long, the sentence that doesn't land, the answer that doesn't quite address the question — is recoverable. But you need to know how to recover before it happens, so the recovery is available under pressure.

Phrases that work precisely because they're short, simple, and retrievable even when complex language isn't:

- "Let me come back to that with more precision in a moment."
- "That's an important question — let me make sure I answer it properly."
- "I want to be precise here, because this matters."
- "Let me restate that more clearly."

These buy time. They signal composure rather than panic. They give you the seconds you need without advertising that you needed them.

### Review performances, not just preparations

After a high-stakes interaction, the most valuable thing you can do is review it while the memory is fresh. What did you say that worked? What felt vague or off? What question caught you completely unprepared?

This review — especially done with someone who can offer honest, specific feedback — closes the gap between your preparation model and what actually happens in real conditions. Without it, the same moments catch you off guard repeatedly.

## The Language Habits That Build Up Under Pressure

There are specific language patterns worth monitoring for in your own speech, because they're the ones pressure amplifies.

**Filler stacking.** "So, I mean, basically, what we're kind of saying is..." — each filler is harmless; together they bury the point.

**Qualifier overload.** "This is a somewhat interesting opportunity in some respects..." — genuine qualification is good; reflexive qualification removes all force from the statement.

**Passive deflection.** "Mistakes were made" / "The deadline wasn't met" — using passive construction to avoid ownership reads as evasion under pressure, regardless of intention.

**Upward intonation on statements.** Ending declarative statements with rising intonation — a pattern from some languages that carries into English — signals uncertainty even when the words are confident.

Identifying which of these patterns is yours — because everyone has one — is the first step to managing it under pressure.

## Closing the Gap

Perfect performance under maximum pressure isn't a realistic goal. Even experienced native-speaking executives have moments where the words don't quite come out right. The goal is to reduce the gap — to bring your high-stakes English closer to your comfortable English.

And the gap does close with deliberate practice. The founders and executives who perform most effectively in English under pressure didn't get there by studying harder. They got there by practising in conditions that challenged them, learning from the moments that didn't work, and building the specific habits that held up when it mattered.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is this just a confidence problem, or is there something specific to work on?**

Both, and they're connected. Confidence in high-stakes contexts builds from performance success — which comes from deliberate practice in conditions that simulate the real thing. Generic confidence advice ("believe in yourself") doesn't produce the kind of context-specific confidence that holds up in a board room. Practise the specific contexts that challenge you, at realistic pressure levels, and confidence follows.

**Does this get better with more years in English-speaking environments?**

Yes — gradually, and unevenly. Passive exposure builds familiarity but doesn't systematically address the performance gap. Active practice, specifically in the high-stakes contexts that are difficult, is significantly faster than waiting for the gap to close on its own.

**What should I do in the moment when I feel my English breaking down?**

Slow down deliberately. The reflex under pressure is to speed up. The productive response is the opposite: slow, deliberate, one sentence at a time. Use a recovery phrase to buy a moment. Return to the simplest statement of your core point. The audience will wait for a clear answer much more comfortably than they'll follow a fast, unclear one.

**How do I know if my preparation is realistic enough?**

If it doesn't feel uncomfortable, it isn't realistic enough. The preparation that produces performance resilience is the kind where you encounter the hard questions, the unexpected challenges, the moments where you don't quite have the answer — and you practise working through them rather than around them.
