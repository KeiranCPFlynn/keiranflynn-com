Clients ask about this regularly, and understandably so. The format question feels like a practical decision with a right answer: which one actually works better?

The honest answer is that for this kind of work, the format difference is real but smaller than most people assume — and it's dwarfed by variables that have nothing to do with where the session happens.

## What Format Differences Are Real

In-person sessions have genuine advantages. The full physical channel is available: posture, presence, the quality of physical stillness or movement that contributes to perceived authority. When the work involves presentations or formal contexts where physical presence matters significantly, working in person allows you to address those elements directly — to observe and adjust how someone carries themselves in a room, not just how they sound on a screen.

There's also something about shared physical space that can make certain kinds of candour easier. Being in the same room with someone who can see you in full tends to sharpen self-awareness. Some clients find that easier to access than the mediated distance of a screen. The conversation has a different quality of presence.

Video sessions have their own genuine advantages. The screen removes some of the ambient social pressure that can make self-consciousness worse in certain clients — many people actually communicate more openly, with less inhibition, on a video call than they would sitting across a table. There's less physical theatre to manage.

Video sessions also more closely replicate the conditions of a significant portion of actual high-stakes work. Investor calls, board meetings with remote members, cross-border negotiations, international partnership conversations — for many professionals, the majority of their highest-stakes English situations happen on a screen. Practising in that format has direct transfer that in-person practice doesn't.

## What Doesn't Change

The core of the work doesn't change with format.

The diagnostic process — understanding the situation, identifying what's actually limiting effectiveness — is conducted through conversation and works identically in both formats. The quality of observation and feedback on how you're communicating is the same.

The feedback mechanism — identifying the specific gap between what you're doing and what would be more effective, and finding the language or approach that closes it — is format-independent.

The rehearsal work, which is where the most value comes from, is if anything more replicable in video sessions, where the client practises in the same environment they'll be performing in for many of their real conversations.

## What Actually Determines Quality

Format is one variable among several, and not the most important. The variables that determine whether coaching produces results:

**The specificity of the objective.** Clients who arrive with a real situation — an investor call next month, a partnership negotiation that's been stalling, a board presentation in six weeks — make significantly more progress than clients who arrive with a general improvement goal. The clarity of purpose focuses everything that follows. Abstract "work on my English" sessions produce abstract results. Specific situation preparation produces specific change.

**The quality of the coaching relationship.** Progress in this kind of work depends on the client being willing to show their real communication — how they perform under actual conditions, not a polished version prepared for the assessment. That willingness is built on trust and safety in the relationship. This has nothing to do with whether the session is online or in person.

**Genuine engagement in the session.** Strategic conversation work requires candour. The practitioner needs to see where things break down, not where they hold up. Clients who work with real material — including material where they're uncertain or underperforming — progress faster. This is a function of the client's engagement, not the format.

**Continuity and timing.** A single session produces something. Ongoing work, especially work that spans a real professional challenge — preparation, performance, and retrospective — produces substantially more. The timing also matters: work anchored to an actual upcoming situation is more effective than work done in the abstract.

## The Format Question That Actually Matters

The useful format question isn't "online or in-person?" It's "what are the actual conditions of the high-stakes communication I'm preparing for?"

If you're preparing for an in-person board presentation where you'll be standing, physically present, and visible in full — then at some point, working in person on the physical elements of presence and authority makes sense. Not because in-person coaching is inherently better, but because the practice environment should match the performance environment.

If you're preparing for an investor call, a pitch to a European partner, a negotiation with a counterpart in a different time zone — working on video is not only fine, it's arguably the more useful format. You're practising in the same channel you'll be performing in.

For most professionals, the honest answer is some version of both — not as a compromise, but because their high-stakes communication genuinely happens in both environments.

## The Practical Realities

**Access and flexibility.** For founders and executives who travel frequently, who are distributed across time zones, or whose schedules don't allow fixed in-person commitments, video sessions remove the friction that interrupts continuity. And continuity — regular sessions anchored to real situations — is what produces results over time.

**Geographic reach.** Phuket has a substantial professional community, and in-person sessions are available for clients based here or visiting. For clients based in other cities or countries, video sessions provide access to the same quality of work without the travel. Several clients work with me across significant time zone differences — the sessions are just as substantive.

**Recording and review.** Video sessions can be recorded (with appropriate consent), which allows clients to review their own communication in a way that in-person sessions typically don't enable. Watching yourself back — particularly in the specific moments that were challenging — is one of the most accelerating forms of feedback available.

## What to Look for When Choosing a Coach

Since format isn't the primary variable, the questions worth asking when choosing a coaching approach:

**Does the work start from your situation, or from a curriculum?** Effective executive coaching in English is entirely driven by what you're working on. A coach with a fixed programme or a set of topics they work through is probably not the right fit for high-stakes professional communication.

**What does feedback actually look like?** Ask for a sample or a description. The feedback should be about whether your communication landed — whether it produced the effect you needed — not about whether sentences were grammatically correct.

**Does the coach have relevant professional experience?** Understanding how business communication actually works — in negotiations, in investor conversations, in leadership contexts — is different from understanding English as a language. Both matter; look for both.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are in-person sessions more expensive than video?**

Pricing is the same for both formats. The value of the session is determined by the quality of the work, not the channel through which it's delivered.

**What setup do I need for a video session?**

A stable internet connection, a quiet space where you're not interrupted, and a camera that shows your face clearly at eye level. Ideally a setup that approximates how you appear on professional calls — not a phone propped on a coffee cup. The closer the practice environment matches your real professional setup, the more directly the practice transfers.

**What if I'm not based near Phuket but want an in-person session?**

A number of clients travel to Phuket for intensive work. For founders and executives facing a significant upcoming challenge — a fundraising round, a major negotiation, an important board meeting — combining focused preparation time with the flexibility of Phuket's working environment can be a productive arrangement. It's worth discussing if the situation warrants it.

**I've had mixed results with online learning in the past. Is this different?**

Almost certainly. The mixed results with online learning typically come from format — asynchronous video courses, automated exercises, self-paced modules — not from the online medium itself. A live, responsive, situation-specific session with genuine two-way dialogue is a completely different experience from an online course, regardless of whether it happens on a screen or in person.
